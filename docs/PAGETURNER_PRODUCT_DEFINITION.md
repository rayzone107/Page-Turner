# PageTurner
## Product Definition Document

> **What this document is:** The single source of truth for what PageTurner is, who it's for, what problem it solves, and what a complete, successful version of it looks like. It does not describe how to build it. It describes what to build and why.

---

## The Idea

PageTurner is a book discovery app that works like Tinder — one book at a time, swipe right to save it, swipe left to pass. But unlike Tinder, it gets smarter with every swipe.

The core insight is that most reading apps fail at discovery. They show you lists, bestseller charts, or genre shelves — all of which require you to already know what you want. PageTurner flips this. You don't search. You react. The app learns from your reactions and progressively narrows in on your actual taste, not just the genres you ticked in a setup screen.

AI is the engine that makes this work. After every ten swipes, Claude reads your entire swipe history and produces a plain-English summary of your reading taste — things like "you gravitate toward morally complex narratives, character-driven, pre-1990, and you consistently skip anything over 500 pages." That profile drives everything: the books you see next, the way each book is described to you, and the occasional curveball pick designed to push your comfort zone.

---

## The Problem It Solves

**"I want to read more, but I can never decide what to read next."**

This is an extremely common and genuinely underserved problem. The decision paralysis of a Goodreads to-read list with 200 books on it is real. The frustration of getting a generic recommendation that doesn't fit your current mood is real. The sense that algorithmic recommendations on Amazon are driven by purchase history rather than literary taste is real.

PageTurner solves this with constraint and personalization. Constraint: you see one book at a time, so you can't defer the decision. Personalization: the app learns your taste from behavior, not from a questionnaire, so it gets more accurate over time rather than stale.

---

## Who It's For

The primary user is a habitual reader — someone who reads regularly but struggles with the gap between finishing one book and starting the next. They have an idea of what they like but can't always articulate it precisely. They want to be surprised occasionally but not constantly. They are comfortable with recommendation systems (they use Spotify, Netflix) but frustrated that no equivalent exists for books.

Secondary audience: lapsed readers who want to get back into reading but feel overwhelmed by choice.

Not the audience: people who already have a methodical reading practice and a clear queue. They don't have the problem this app solves.

## The Product, Screen by Screen

### Onboarding (3 taps, then done)

The first time the app opens, the user answers two questions:

1. **Which genres interest you?** — Multi-select chip grid. Options include: Literary Fiction, Science Fiction, Fantasy, Thriller, Historical Fiction, Mystery, Non-Fiction, Biography, Horror, Philosophy, Classics, Short Stories. The user must pick at least one. This is the cold-start seed — without it, the first cards have no basis.

2. **How long do you like your books?** — Three options: Short (under 200 pages), Medium (200–400 pages), Long (400+ pages). Single select.

That's it. No account creation. No email. No name. The app starts immediately after these two taps. The onboarding is intentionally minimal — the real personalization happens through swiping, not through a survey.

---

### Screen 1: Swipe Deck (the main experience)

This is the home screen. It shows a stack of book cards — you can see the top card in full and the edges of two cards behind it, creating depth.

**Each card shows:**
- The book's cover art (sourced from Open Library cover images)
- Title and author
- Publication year
- Page count
- Up to 3 genre/subject chips
- A match score bar — a visual indicator of how well this book aligns with the current taste profile
- A 2-sentence AI brief — not the raw Open Library description, but a rewritten, personalized hook. This brief is generated by Claude using the user's taste profile as context. Someone who loves dark, morally complex fiction sees a different brief for the same book than someone who loves worldbuilding and escapism.
- If the card is a Wildcard pick (see below), a teal "Wildcard pick" badge appears on the card

**The three actions:**
- Swipe left (or tap X) — Skip. "Not for me." This book goes to the back of the all-time pool and is significantly deprioritized.
- Swipe right (or tap checkmark) — Add to reading list. Saved to the user's list with full metadata.
- Tap the bookmark icon — Save for later. A softer save — "I'm curious but not committing." Goes to a separate "Maybe" pile.

**The counter:** A subtle line at the bottom of the screen shows "X swipes · profile updating in Y." This makes the AI learning visible and gives users a small sense of progress.

---

### Screen 2: Taste Profile

Accessible from the bottom nav. This screen makes the AI's understanding of the user legible.

**It shows:**
- The AI-generated taste summary — a quoted paragraph in Claude's own words, e.g. *"Dark, morally complex stories. Character-driven. Pre-1990. You avoid romance and anything over 600 pages."* This is Claude's literal output, stored verbatim and displayed directly.
- What you love — chips for the genres/themes that appear most in right-swipes
- What you tend to skip — chips for genres/lengths that correlate with left-swipes
- Swipe stats — total swiped, total saved, and wildcard books kept (this last number is meaningful — it measures how adventurous the user is and how well the wildcard system is working)
- When the profile was last updated

This screen serves two purposes. First, it's intrinsically satisfying — people enjoy seeing themselves reflected accurately. Second, it builds trust. When the app shows you a book and it feels right, you can come here to understand why. The AI isn't a mystery; it's a mirror.

---

### Screen 3: Reading List

The user's saved books. A clean grid of cover art — visually rich, minimal text. Tapping any cover opens the Book Detail screen.

The reading list is the app's output — the thing the user takes away from the experience. It should feel like a curated personal shelf, not a database table.

The list works fully offline. Once a book is saved, all its data (metadata, cover, AI brief) is cached locally. The user can browse their reading list on a plane.

The empty state shows a illustrated bare shelf with the line: *"Your next great read is waiting."* It is never a blank screen with a gray message.

---

### Screen 4: Book Detail

Tapping any book — from the swipe deck (via an "expand" tap rather than a swipe) or from the reading list — opens this screen.

**It shows:**
- Full-width hero cover image
- Title, author, year, page count
- Subject chips (all of them, scrollable)
- Full description (expandable — truncated to 3 lines with a "Show more" tap)
- The AI brief in full
- The wildcard explanation, if applicable ("We picked this because you've loved complex moral choices — this book approaches that from a completely different angle")
- A link to Open Library to read more or find where to get the book

This screen has no save/skip action — by the time the user gets here, they've already made a swipe decision. It's a deeper look, not a decision point.

## The AI System — What It Does and When

There are three distinct AI jobs in PageTurner. Each is a separate Claude API call with a separate purpose.

### Job 1: Brief Generator
**When:** Once per book, the first time a book enters a user's queue.
**Input:** Book metadata (title, author, year, subjects, description, page count) + the user's current taste profile summary.
**Output:** Two sentences. A personalized hook that reframes the book through the lens of what this specific user cares about.
**Why this is AI's job:** A rule engine cannot rewrite prose in a contextually sensitive way. The brief must feel like it was written for you, not copy-pasted from the publisher's description.
**What it is not:** A summary of the plot. It is a pitch — why *you* might love this book.

### Job 2: Profile Summarizer
**When:** Triggered automatically after every 10 swipes (i.e., after swipe 10, 20, 30, etc.).
**Input:** The full swipe history — each swipe with its direction (left/right/bookmark), the book's genres, era, length, and whether it was a wildcard pick.
**Output:** A structured taste profile object containing: liked genres, avoided genres, preferred length range, recurring themes, and a plain-English summary paragraph (1–3 sentences).
**Why this is AI's job:** Pattern recognition across mixed signals (genre, length, era, themes, wildcard behavior) and translation of those patterns into articulate natural language is exactly what language models do well. A rule engine would give you a genre frequency table. Claude gives you a sentence a human could have written.
**What it is not:** A recommendation. This job is purely analytical — it reads the data and describes what it sees.

### Job 3: Wildcard Picker
**When:** Every 7th card in the swipe deck.
**Input:** The user's current taste profile + a list of candidate books from genres not in the user's top preferences.
**Output:** One specific book selection, plus a 1-sentence explanation of why this particular book was chosen as the wildcard (e.g., "You love moral ambiguity — this literary fiction pick delivers it in a completely different register than your usual sci-fi").
**Why this is AI's job:** A random genre pick is jarring and often alienating. A thoughtful wildcard pick that shares a quality the user demonstrably loves — but expresses it through an unfamiliar genre — is the difference between discovery and noise. Only a model that understands the profile can make that distinction.
**What it is not:** Random. The whole point is that it's intentional. The wildcard explanation on the card surface makes this explicit.

## What the App Learns Over Time

The personalization is progressive. The experience on session one is decent. The experience on session five is noticeably better. This is deliberate and visible.

**Sessions 1–2 (0–20 swipes):** The queue is seeded primarily from the genres selected in onboarding. The AI brief is generated but not yet deeply personalized — the profile is too thin. The match score is present but less meaningful.

**Sessions 3–5 (20–50 swipes):** The profile summarizer has now run 2–5 times. Genre preferences are becoming clear. Length preference is confirmed or updated. The briefs are noticeably more targeted. The wildcard picks are starting to make sense.

**Sessions 6+ (50+ swipes):** The profile is rich. The briefs feel like they were written by someone who knows you. Wildcards feel adventurous rather than random. The Taste Profile screen is satisfying to read. The reading list is a genuine reflection of the user's literary identity.

This arc — from generic to personal — is the product's central promise, and every design and engineering decision should be evaluated against it.

## What the App Does Not Do

These are explicit non-goals. They are not features that were cut for time — they are features that would dilute the product's core value.

- **No user accounts or authentication.** Everything is local. Privacy is the default.
- **No social features.** No following, no sharing, no public profiles. Reading is personal.
- **No ratings input.** The user does not rate books they've read. The only signal is the swipe direction. Adding a rating UI muddies the simplicity of the interaction and creates a secondary chore.
- **No star ratings from external sources.** Open Library does not reliably expose Goodreads scores. The match score shown on cards is calculated locally from profile alignment — it is not an external quality signal. This is a deliberate choice: the app is calibrating to *your* taste, not to crowd consensus.
- **No reading progress tracking.** That is a different app (Goodreads, StoryGraph). This app's job ends when the user has a reading list. What they do with it is their business.
- **No push notifications.** The app does not reach out. The user comes to it when they want to discover.

## Data Sources

**Open Library API** (`https://openlibrary.org`)
Public domain. No authentication required. Used for:
- Book search by subject/genre: `/search.json?subject={genre}&limit=20&page={n}`
- Book detail: `/works/{workId}.json`
- Cover images: `https://covers.openlibrary.org/b/id/{coverId}-M.jpg`

**Claude API** (`claude-sonnet-4-20250514`)
Used for the three AI jobs described above. The app includes an API key in the build config. API calls are made from the Android app directly — no intermediate backend is required.

**On-device storage (Room / SQLite)**
All user data lives on the device:
- Swipe history
- Taste profile (updated by the AI summarizer)
- Reading list (saved books with full metadata)
- Brief cache (generated briefs, keyed by book + profile version, to avoid regenerating)
- Seen books index (to prevent the same book appearing twice)

## Definition of Done

The app is complete when a first-time user can:

1. Open the app, complete onboarding in under 30 seconds
2. See a swipe deck of real books with real covers, real metadata, and AI-generated briefs
3. Swipe through at least 10 books and have the experience feel responsive and fluid
4. See their taste profile update and reflect their swipe behavior accurately
5. Have a wildcard card appear and understand why it was suggested
6. Find all right-swiped books on their reading list
7. Open a book detail and see the full information
8. Close the app, reopen it, and find their reading list and taste profile exactly as they left them
9. Turn off wifi, reopen the app, and still be able to browse their reading list

The app is also complete when an interviewer can read the codebase and see:
- Clear multi-module architecture with enforced boundaries
- MVI pattern applied consistently across all screens
- AI integration that is clearly separated from UI and business logic
- Unit tests covering all non-trivial logic
- A design system that every screen draws from, with no hardcoded values
- Git history that shows the CRAFT agent workflow in action
